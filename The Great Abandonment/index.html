The Great Abandonment

A story of the ones left behind.

Chapter 1: The Great Abandonment

The sky wasn't black; it was the color of a fresh bruise—deep purples and sickly oranges, choked with the first taste of the ash. Elara stood on the roof of the "Signal Nest," her boots crunching on a thin layer of grey grit that hadn't been there two hours ago. In her hand, she gripped a heavy-duty, weathered multimeter like a talisman. Below her, the city of Oakhaven was a ghost of its former self. The traffic hadn't jammed; it had simply stopped. People hadn't fought in the streets; they had gone home to wait for a miracle that wasn't coming. Then, the first roar started. It wasn't the volcanoes—not yet. It was the ships. From the private launch pads twenty miles East, three silver needles pierced the heavy haze, riding pillars of white-hot flame. The "Vanguard" class vessels. They were beautiful, sleek, and carried every person who had ever signed Elara’s paychecks, every politician who had promised a solution, and every billionaire who had spent the last decade building a lifeboat instead of a lighthouse. "Cowards," she whispered, the word tasting like sulfur on her tongue. She didn't watch them until they disappeared. She didn't have the time. She turned her back on the ascending gods and dropped to her knees beside the base of the massive steel radio tower that dominated the roof. The "Convergence Alert" was still screaming from the emergency sirens below, a mechanical wail that set her teeth on edge. It was a countdown to a funeral. The sensors were clear: the crust was thinning. Every dormant vent from the Pacific Ring to the hidden hotspots under the plains was preparing to exhale at once. The "leaders" had seen the data first, and they had used that head start to pack their bags. Elara pulled a heavy canvas roll from her belt and spread it out. Inside were her tools—not the sleek, touch-screen diagnostics of the modern era, but the heavy, iron-clad survivors: a high-wattage soldering gun, a set of precision drivers with worn grips, and a spool of high-grade copper wire. "If the world is going to end," she muttered, adjusting her goggles as the wind began to howl, "it’s going to happen while I’m mid-weld." She opened the junction box at the tower's base. The modern digital relays were already flickering, overwhelmed by the electromagnetic interference of the shifting atmosphere. They were too delicate, too "smart" for a world that was becoming "dumb." With a practiced flick, Elara began bypassing the circuit boards. She wasn't trying to save the internet. She wasn't trying to save the cloud. She was stripping the system down to its bones. She was building a bridge made of vacuum tubes and raw voltage—something that could punch through the coming fire. A deep, subterranean thud shook the building. It wasn't a sound; it was a vibration that traveled through her boots and into her teeth. The first of the local vents had cracked. "Not yet," she hissed, her fingers moving with a blurred, mechanical grace. She stripped a wire, looped it, and tightened a terminal. "Just give me ten minutes of stability." She looked up. The silver needles in the sky were gone, safely beyond the atmosphere, leaving five million "expendables" to face the heat. She felt a strange, cold clarity. They had the ships, the hibernation pods, and the high-tech silence of space. But she had the tools. And as she clicked the final manual bypass into place and the old-school analog dial on her transmitter hummed to life, Elara realized she had something else they didn't. She had the floor. The world didn't end with a bang; it ended with a cough. Three days had passed since the Great Abandonment. The massive eruptions that were supposed to turn the crust into a molten marble had sputtered into a series of jagged, violent gasps. The pressure had vented just enough to keep the planet in one piece, but it had left the atmosphere thick with a heavy, conductive grey veil. The sun was a pale, ghost-white coin behind a curtain of ash that never stopped falling. Elara sat in the center of the Signal Nest, her face smudged with grease and carbon. The world was terrifyingly quiet. No planes, no sirens, no hum of a distant power grid. Just the rhythmic tink-tink-tink of cooling metal in her workshop. "Day three," she muttered, clicking a heavy toggle switch. Her workshop was a fortress of "old-world" tech. She had spent the last seventy-two hours stripping the high-frequency digital gear that was now useless—fried by the massive static charges in the air—and replacing it with thick-walled vacuum tubes and copper coils the size of dinner plates. She leaned into the microphone, a heavy, brushed-steel relic from the 1970s. "This is Oakhaven Station. North America. Frequency 14.250. Is anyone there?" Nothing but the roar of the atmosphere. The ash in the air acted like a giant blanket, soaking up radio waves. To get through, she didn't need "smart" tech; she needed brute force. She stood up and walked over to her power bank. The city's electricity had died an hour after the ships left, but Elara had planned for that. She had rigged a series of old truck alternators to a makeshift steam turbine powered by the heat of a nearby volcanic vent that had cracked through the street two blocks away. "Come on, you beautiful disaster," she whispered, checking the analog gauges. "Give me the juice." She turned a heavy iron wheel, venting steam into the turbine. The room began to vibrate. The lightbulbs—old filaments, not LEDs—flickered into a warm, amber glow. She returned to the radio, her hands hovering over the tuning knob. She started at the bottom of the band and began to turn. Static. Whine. The crackle of a lightning strike five hundred miles away. Static. Then, a sound. It wasn't a voice. It was a series of rhythmic clicks. Three short, three long, three short. "Morse," Elara breathed. "Somebody’s using a telegraph key." She grabbed her own key—a brass lever she’d polished until it shone—and tapped back. R-D-R. (Received). The clicks stopped. There was a long, agonizing pause where the only sound was the wind howling against the tower outside. Then, the speaker erupted in a burst of distorted, human audio. "...lo? ...is... Cape Town... hearing... anyone?" Elara’s heart hammered against her ribs. She grabbed the mic, her thumb white-knuckled on the PTT button. "Cape Town, this is Oakhaven! I hear you! You’re coming through at a strength of three. Who is this?" "...name is Elias," the voice crackled, sounding thin and exhausted but undeniably alive. "...thought... I was the only one left. The ships... they're gone, Elara. They really left us." "They left us," Elara confirmed, her eyes landing on her heavy soldering iron, still hot on the bench. "But they left the keys in the ignition, Elias. And they left the people who know how to fix things." "What do we do?" Elias asked. Elara looked out the window at the ash-covered horizon. Somewhere up there, the "leaders" were sleeping in sterile, silver tubes, waiting for the world to die so they could claim the bones. "We talk," Elara said, her voice growing steady. "We find every single frequency. We map the survivors. And then, we start building a world they won't recognize when they come back." The "Voice of Elias" had changed everything. It was no longer just survival; it was a deadline. If Elias was in Cape Town and she was in Oakhaven, the "Bridge" between them was fragile, held together by luck and a few glowing vacuum tubes. To reach the rest of the five million, she needed more than a workshop—she needed a Broadcaster’s Heart. "The signal is dropping, Elias," Elara said into the mic, watching the needle on her output meter dance toward the red. "The ash is ionizing. It’s creating a ceiling. If I don’t increase the wattage, the atmosphere is going to swallow us whole." "...understand," Elias’s voice was fading into a sea of white noise. "...be careful, Elara. The ground... it isn't stable." The radio went to dead static. Elara didn't hesitate. She threw on her heavy duster coat—a thick, waxed canvas that shed ash like water—and strapped on her respirator. She grabbed her "Go-Bag," a rugged kit filled with specialized wrenches, a portable voltmeter, and her favorite cordless oscillating saw. Her target was the Oakhaven Telecom Hub, three miles into the city center. It was a brutalist concrete tower that housed the old-school microwave relays. More importantly, it housed a Model-7 Power Amplifier—a piece of tech so heavy and "inefficient" that the elites had left it behind in favor of satellite chips. To Elara, it was pure gold. She stepped out of the Signal Nest and into the "Grey." The city was a monochromatic nightmare. Everything—the cars, the trees, the corpses of abandoned storefronts—was draped in six inches of fine, volcanic silt. It muffled every sound, turning the world into a sensory deprivation chamber. She moved with the gait of a builder, eyes scanning the structural integrity of the buildings she passed. She didn't look for food; she looked for copper. She saw a downed power line and stopped for three minutes to strip the high-gauge wire. In this new world, copper was more valuable than diamonds. It was the nervous system of her new global body. Halfway to the hub, the ground groaned. It wasn't a tremor; it was a settling. A section of the street fifty yards ahead simply vanished into a sinkhole, swallowed by a collapsed subway tunnel. A plume of hot, sulfuric steam hissed upward. "Easy, girl," she whispered to the Earth. "Just stay still for an hour." She reached the Telecom Hub just as the wind began to pick up, swirling the ash into blinding "dust-devils." The front glass doors were shattered. Inside, the lobby was a tomb of paperwork and dead computer monitors. Elara ignored the elevators and headed for the stairs. Twelve flights. Her lungs burned through the respirator filters, but she didn't stop until she reached the Maintenance Level. There, bolted to the floor in a room shielded by lead-lined walls, sat the beast. The Model-7. It was a rack-mounted monster, half the size of a refrigerator, covered in cooling fins and manual dials. "Found you," she breathed. She knelt and opened her tool kit. This wasn't a job for a crowbar; it was surgery. She had to disconnect the high-voltage capacitors without blowing her own heart out, then extract the central core. As she worked, the silence of the building was broken. Clack. Clack. Clack. Someone—or something—was in the stairwell. Elara froze, her hand gripping a heavy pipe wrench. She wasn't a fighter, but she was a builder, and she knew exactly how much force it took to break a connection. "I know you're there," a voice called out. It wasn't a "leader" voice. It was rough, young, and terrified. "I saw your lights on the roof across town. You’re the Radio Girl." Elara didn't lower the wrench. "I'm the one with the tools. Who are you?" A lanky teenager stepped into the doorway, clutching a vintage handheld walkie-talkie like a holy relic. "My name is Kael. My dad... he was a technician here. He told me if the world ever went dark, someone would come for the Model-7." He looked at the machine, then at Elara. "Are you going to turn the lights back on?" Elara looked at the massive amplifier, then back at the boy. She saw the same hunger for connection in his eyes that she felt every time she turned the tuning knob. "No," Elara said, handing him a screwdriver. "I'm going to do something better. I'm going to make sure we’re never quiet again. Grab the other side of the housing. We’ve got a world to wire up." The trek back was a grueling test of physics. The Model-7 core weighed nearly eighty pounds of dead-weight copper and lead. Elara and Kael carried it between them on a makeshift litter made of heavy-duty cabling and a repurposed metal door. Every step through the ash was a struggle. They moved like ghosts through the monochromatic ruins of Oakhaven, their breathing heavy and rhythmic inside their respirators. Elara kept her eyes moving, scanning the rooftops. In the silence of the "Grey," she felt a prickle on the back of her neck—the sensation of being watched. "Don't look back," she hissed to Kael as they crested the final rise toward the Signal Nest. "Just keep your grip. If we drop this, the vacuum tubes crack, and we’re back to shouting into the wind." They reached the heavy steel door of the Nest's base. Elara punched in a mechanical code—her own bypass of the now-dead electronic lock—and they slipped inside. They hauled the Model-7 up the service stairs, their muscles screaming, until they finally slammed the workshop door shut and bolted the three heavy manual deadbolts Elara had installed. "We made it," Kael panted, collapsing against a workbench. "We actually got it." Elara didn't celebrate. She went straight to the window. Down in the street, three figures emerged from the swirling ash. They weren't wearing respirators; they had tattered cloths wrapped around their faces. They weren't looking for food or water. They were staring straight up at the Signal Nest's antenna tower. "They followed the light," Elara whispered. "They don't want to talk to the world, Kael. They want the power to control who gets heard." She turned to her workbench. "We have to wire this in. Now. If they get in here, I want a signal already out. I want the world to know what’s happening." She worked with a feverish, desperate speed. Kael held the leads while Elara soldered connections with a shaking hand. The Model-7 groaned as she fed it the raw, unrefined current from her steam-driven turbine. The vacuum tubes began to glow—not a warm amber, but a fierce, violet-white. Suddenly, the heavy steel door of the workshop shuddered. BOOM. A sledgehammer. Then the screech of a power saw—one of her own tools, likely scavenged from a lower floor. "They’re cutting through!" Kael yelled, backing away from the door. Elara didn't look up. "Hold the ground wire! Don't let go!" She jammed the final lead into the transmitter. The Model-7 roared to life with a high-pitched hum that made the glass jars on her shelves vibrate. She grabbed the brushed-steel mic. "This is Oakhaven! To anyone on this frequency! The Nest is under attack! We have the amplifier, we have the reach, but we are—" The door's top hinge snapped with a deafening crack. A gap appeared in the steel. A face appeared in the opening—wild eyes, soot-stained skin. "Give us the box, Tech-Girl!" the man screamed over the roar of the saw. "We see the ships coming back! We know you’re talking to 'em! We want our ticket out!" They didn't understand. They thought she was negotiating for a seat on a ship that was never coming back for them. "Kael, the secondary bag! Now!" Elara commanded. She didn't try to save the workshop. She grabbed the "Super-Transmitter"—a compact, over-tuned unit she had just finished slaving to the Model-7’s core. It was the size of a suitcase, trailing wires like umbilical cords. "We can't stay!" she yelled over the splintering wood of the door. She kicked out the back window—the one leading to the old fire escape that hung precariously over a five-story drop into a narrow, ash-choked alley. "The radio?" Kael cried, looking at the glowing, massive Model-7. "It's a beacon now!" Elara shouted, shoving him toward the window. "It’ll broadcast until they smash it! Move!" As the workshop door finally gave way with a roar of triumph from the scavengers, Elara leaped onto the rusted iron slats of the fire escape. Behind her, the room exploded into chaos. She and Kael scrambled down the shaking metal stairs, the heavy suitcase-radio banging against her ribs. Below them, the alley was a dark, narrow throat filled with shadows. Above them, the scavengers realizes she’d escaped with the "brain" of the operation and began to howl. Elara hit the ground running, her boots kicking up clouds of grey soot. "Where are we going?" Kael screamed as they sprinted into the blinding ash storm, the sounds of pursuit closing in from both ends of the alley. "We keep the signal alive!" Elara yelled, her hand tightening on the handle of the transmitter. "Run!" In the distance, through the thick veil of the volcanic sky, a single, bright spark of light appeared. It wasn't a star. It was the first ship, beginning its descent.
